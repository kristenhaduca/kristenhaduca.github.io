WNBA Salaries & Statistics [EXCEL]

A data exploration into the relationship between salaries and game statistics of players in the WNBA using Excel. Linear regression analysis and hypothesis testing is done to see relationships between the two.

PROGRAM USED: EXCEL
SKILLS USED: #Data Scraping, #Power Query, #Data Cleaning, #Data Wrangling, #Central Tendency & Dispersion, #Visualization, #Linear Regression Analysis, #Hypothesis Testing
MAIN TOOLS USED: PowerQuery, Data Analysis ToolPak, Find and Select, Conditional Formatting, Tables (including filtering), Scatter Plot Chart, Column Chart, PivotTable (with slicers), PivotChart
MAIN FORMULAS USED: INDEX(), MATCH(), IFS(), SUMIFS(), COUNTIFS(), AVERAGEIF(), ISERROR(), AGGREGATE(), AVERAGE(), MEDIAN(), MAX(), MIN(), MODE(), VAR.S(), STDEV.S(), LINEST(), TINV(), QUARTILE.INC()

PART 1: DATA SOURCE & DATA SCRAPING USING POWER QUERY

The 2021 salary data for all players of each team is scraped using Power Query from Her Hoop Stats and made into individual team tables.

The 2020 game statistics for all players are also scraped using Power Query from Her Hoop Stats and made into one table.

PART 2: DATA CLEANING & WRANGLING

Salaries Table

All of the individual team salary tables are merged into one main worksheet (WNBA Main) and a main salary table. All of the salaries are changed to currency format. Since the project is looking at 2020 and 2021 salaries, and Her Hoops Stats only has the salaries tables going back to 2021 at earliest, 2020 salary data is manually added from another page of Her Hoop Stats where there are additional images listing salary data for 2020. Excess columns with future years are removed.

2020 Statistics Table

Power Query included repeated player name information under the Player column, so this excess text is split into a new column using Text to Columns with the semicolon delimiter (this can also be done within Power Query itself):

Data tab > Text to Columns > Delimited by Semicolon

Combining Both Tables Into One Table

The game statistics of interest (Games Played, Minutes Per Game, and Points Per Game) from the 2020 Statistics Table are appended to the Salary Table on the WNBA Main worksheet using INDEX() and MATCH() to index the statistics and match them by player name. These functions are nested within the IF() and ISERROR() functions to return a blank cell if there is an #N/A error after the INDEX()/MATCH()—this error being caused by no statistics information in the 2020 Statistics Table because of things such as the player having been in college or not playing during the 2020 season. The table containing both the salaries and statistics will now be referred to as the Full WNBA Table.

=IF(ISERROR(INDEX(Stats_2020[Games played],MATCH([@Player],Stats_2020[Player],0))),"",(INDEX(Stats_2020[Games played],MATCH([@Player],Stats_2020[Player],0))))

At this point, I noticed some discrepancies between the 2021 salaries on some players listed on the salaries page and on the 2020 statistics page of Her Hoops Stats. Working on the WNBA Main worksheet, I add a new column indexing the 2021 salaries listed per player on the 2020 Statistics Table using INDEX() and MATCH().

=INDEX('2020 Stats'!$C$2:$C$128,MATCH('WNBA MAIN'!$B2,'2020 Stats'!$A$2:$A$128,0))

These two values are compared using IF()—labeled "Same" if they are the same, and "Different" if they are different. #N/A errors designate those players without salaries listed on the 2020 statistics page.

=IF($D2=$K2,"Same","Different")

Find & Select is used to find all cells with "Different" and highlight them (Conditional Formatting can also be used instead).

After researching the players with discrepencies, I manually update the 2021 salary figures to the best results from my research.

Adding Position and WNBA Experience Data

New tables are manually copy and pasted from the official WNBA's website's team roster lists and are reformatted into tables on each teams' individual worksheets.

Postion data is appended to the Full WNBA Table using INDEX() and MATCH().

=INDEX('Atlanta Dream'!$L$2:$L$16,MATCH('WNBA MAIN'!$B2,'Atlanta Dream'!$K$2:$K$16,0))

Some player information was missing and giving #N/A errors. These new tables are updated by manually adding Position and Experience data taken from individual player profile pages.

Then, WNBA experience data is also added to the Full WNBA Table using INDEX() and MATCH().

=INDEX('Atlanta Dream'!$P$2:$P$16,MATCH('WNBA MAIN'!$B2,'Atlanta Dream'!$K$2:$K$16,0))

Adding Missing Data for Big Players

Because the 2020 WNBA season took place inside the pandemic Bubble, many key players did not play due to health concerns. I acknowledge that mixing 2019 stats with 2020 stats would skew the data a bit (for example, certain players who are filling in for these players would have altered stats in 2020 that they wouldn't have in previous years due to limited playing time). While it would be best practice to use a comparison of 2018 and 2019 stats to give a more accurate picture of a normal season, for the sake of this project's scope and including the newer, evolving salary negotiations—in addition to there only being a handful of players (all important WNBA figures whose absence in this data would be remiss) who opted out—it was easiest to include their 2019 stats with the rest of the 2020 stats to give a general overlook.

These players are included in the 2021 salary data, but have blanks within the 2020 statistics data. A new column containing an IF() statement is used to find those players that had a salary listed but no stats from 2020—and to differentiate them from the rookies coming out of college. If the Games Played column cell is blank and the 2020 Salary column cell is "College", then "College" will be listed. If the Games Played column cell is blank and the 2020 Salary column cell is a number (using ISNUMBER()), then "Blank" will be listed—the desired outcome for filtering those players who have salaries listed but no stats data. Otherwise, both salary and stats data is fully listed (or are listed as waived, suspended, or otherwise did not play and will not be looked at later in this project) and a 0 will be returned.

=IF(AND($E2="",$C2="College"),"College",IF(AND($E2="",ISNUMBER($C2)),"Blank",0))

Conditional Formatting is used with a FIND() formula to highlight in red cells that contain "Blank" (with cell N2 being a reference cell containing "Blank").

Conditional Formatting > New Rule > Use a formula to determine which cells to format > Set formatting to red fill

The 2019 stats are retrieved from individual player profile pages and manually added to the 2020 Statistics page for those players flagged.

PART 3: FILTERING, CENTRAL TENDENCY, & DISPERSION CALCULATIONS

In this section, filter options are used on the Full WNBA Table in order to look at interesting subgroups of players and do Central Tendency and Dispersion calculations. Each filtered tables are copy and pasted into their own individual worksheets.

Veterans (5/+ years experience playing in the WNBA)

Central tendency and dispersion calculations are done using AVERAGE(), MEDIAN(), MAX(), MIN(), MODE(), VAR.S(), and STDEV.S().

Median salary (not affected by large outliers in data) for veterans in the WNBA is about $118,510 in 2020 and $167,500 in 2021. This increase in median salary between the two years suggests more veterans had their salaries increased in 2021 (as opposed to only a few outlier top players getting paid more).

The maximum salary for veterans in the WNBA is about $215,000 in 2020 and $221,450 in 2021.

NOTE: Minimum salary is not fully accurate—WNBA has a minimum salary floor for players with 3+ years experience at $70,040 in 2021 (source: Her Hoops Stats), but some players are listed on the site with a lower salary (due to not actually playing, being contract players, etc). These inaccurate values slightly skew the data, but for the simplistic scope of this project, it gives a general overview of the figures and does not significantly alter the data too much. Additionally, any blank cells are automatically ignored in Excel's computations.

Average salary (affected by large outliers and thus, less accurate for this data set because of the few inaccurate, outlier salaries reported) for veterans in the WNBA is about $136,888 in 2020 and $155,559 in 2021, with a standard deviation from the mean of about $42,843 in 2020 and $47,129 in 2021.

Rookies (First year in the WNBA)

NOTE: I am including all the players that have the "R" under their experience as listed on the official WNBA website's team rosters. There are some players here that have odd inaccuracies and aren't listed as being "College" for their 2020 salaries despite being supposedly listed as a rookie on the official website, but again, for the sake of the scope of this project I will include all the players the WNBA has listed as a rookie. Excel ignores entries with blanks when doing computations, so those with blanks will not alter anything.

Central tendency and dispersion calculations are done using AVERAGE(), MEDIAN(), MAX(), MIN(), MODE(), VAR.S(), and STDEV.S().

Median salary (not affected by large outliers in data) for rookies in the WNBA is about $61,543 in 2021.

The maximum salary for rookies in the WNBA in 2021 is about $70,040.

NOTE: The previous note applies again—the WNBA has a minimum salary floor for rookies at $58,710 in 2021 (source: Her Hoops Stats).

Average salary (affected by large outliers) for rookies in the WNBA is about $49,716 in 2021, with a standard deviation of about $25,551.

All Players in WNBA

Central tendency and dispersion calculations are done using AGGREGATE() (an alternate to using each individual formula) and MODE().

Median salary for all WNBA players (not affected by large outliers) is $88,850 in 2020 and $110,000 in 2021. This increase suggests more players had been paid higher salaries in 2021, compared to 2020.

The maximum salary for all WNBA players is $215,000 in 2020 and $221,450 in 2021.

NOTE: The previous note applies once again—the minimum salary listed is not entirely accurate because WNBA has a minimum salary floor for players with 0-2 years experience at $58,710 in 2021 and players with 3+ years experience at $70,040 in 2021.

Average salary (affected by large outliers) for all WNBA players is about $103,557 in 2020 and $117,880 in 2021, with a standard deviation of about $50,026 in 2020 and $58,910 in 2021.

An additional pivot table is created with each WNBA team and their average 2021 salaries. The number format is changed to currency and a slicer is added to easily select the team.

First, simple linear regressions are run for each game statistic to see the relationship between each individual statistic and salary. Then, multiple linear regression is run to find out which statistic is a better predictor of salary. NOTE: I treat the statistics that were taken from 2020 as a constant between 2020 and 2021 for these regressions for simplicity's sake.

MULTIPLE LINEAR REGRESSIONS:

To identify which game statistics are better predictors of salary, a correlation matrix between all the variables is created, and then multiple linear regression is run.

1. Correlation Matrix:

A correlation matrix is created to show the linear correlation between each pair of variables and to identify any multicollinearity issues. The independent variables (game statistics) should have low correlations with each other and a high correlation with the dependent variable (salary). The correlation matrix is made using the Correlation option in the Data Analysis ToolPak.

Data tab > Data Analysis > Correlation

2020:

The game stats do not have particularly high correlation with salary, with Points Per Game being the highest at 0.428. Minutes Per Game and Points Per Game have a high correlation with each other (if a player plays more minutes, they are more likely to also score more points), so it does not make much sense to include both of these variables. Because Minutes Per Game has the lowest correlation with salary, it will be dropped prior to running the regression.

2021:

Minutes Per Game and Points Per Game have a stronger correlation with salary in 2021, compared to 2020. Games Played continues to have a low correlation with salary. Minutes Per Game and Points Per Game also continue to have a high correlation with each other because we are taking these statistics as constant both years—Minutes Per Game is redundant and will be dropped again.

2. Multiple Linear Regression:

2020:

Because the Data Analysis Toolpak's Regression option cannot use input data that is not a contiguous reference, a new table is created with only the relevant data (Salary, Games Played, and Points Per Game).

Then, the regression with Salary, Games Played, and Points Per Game is run through the Data Analysis ToolPak:

            Hypotheses:
                  Games Played:
                        H0: b1=0
                        H1: b1≠0
                  Points Per Game:
                              H0: b2=0
                              H1: b2≠0
            Multiple Regression Equation: Salary = -791.497GP + 4340.495PPG+102990.227
                    If games played increased by 1 game, salary would decrease by $791.50 on average, assuming points per game is constant. If points per game increased by 1 point, salary would increase by $4,340.50 on average, assuming games played is constant.
            p-values:
                  Significance F: F=0.003 < 0.05
                  Games Played: p=0.345 > 0.05
                  Points Per Game: p=0.0007 < 0.05
Interpretation:
            Significance F: The regression model as a whole, with explanatory variables Games Played and Points Per Game combined, have a statistically significant association with Salary (F=0.003 < 0.05).
            Games Played: We fail to reject the null hypothesis that salary and games played are not related (p=0.345 > 0.05). There is not enough evidence for a statistically significant relationship between salary and games played. Because of the high p-value, the number of games played has poor predictive ability. Logically, it also does not make sense that playing more games would result in a lower salary.
            Points Per Game: We reject the null hypothesis that salary and points per game are not related (p=0.0007 < 0.05). There is a statistically significant positive relationship between salary and points per game. As points per game increases, salary also tends to increase.

Because Games Played has a high p-value, it is removed from the regression and the regression is run again with just Points Per Game. The result is the same as the previous simple linear regression with it by itself. Points Per Game is the best predictor of Salary in this instance.

Discussion

This exploration looked at the salaries of WNBA players, and at the relationships between salaries and three game statistics (Games Played, Minutes Per Game, and Points Per Game). More granular exploration was also done to see the differences between veterans (5/+ years experience in the WNBA) and the entirety of players in the WNBA.

In 2020, median salary was $118,510 for veterans and $88,850 for all WNBA players. In 2021, median salary was $167,500 for veterans and $110,00 for all WNBA players. As one would expect, veterans have a higher median salary than the general WNBA due to them having more experience and, therefore, more of them getting paid on the higher range of salaries. With increases in team budgets afforded to the WNBA league in 2021, median salaries increased between 2020 and 2021—showing that more players were getting higher salaries (as opposed to only veterans or a few star players getting salary increases).

Veterans tend to have a stronger relationship between their salaries and their game statistics than the general WNBA. In 2021, the percent of variation in salary that is explained by variation in Minutes Per Game was 33.6% for the veterans and 23.02% in the general WNBA. In 2021, the percent of variation in salary that is explained by variation in Points Per Game was 44% for veterans and 23.2% in the general WNBA. However, correlation does not equal causation, and there could be a lot of different reasons why there are relationships between salary and the various game stats. For the veterans, it could be that veterans tend to have the most experience so they get the highest salaries while also playing the most time and scoring the most points—the relationship between salary and these stats are tighter because those contributing the most for the longest amount of time get compensated for that effort. The relationship between salary and the game stats might be weaker in the general WNBA players group because there is such a spread of who gets to play (limited playing time with only five players on the court at once), how well they perform when they actually play (if they are brought in from the bench, are they consistent enough in their performance to have their skills accurately portrayed?), and the base salaries they can get. Due to salary budgets, players can not get up to the supermax salaries veterans can receive until they reach a certain number of years played; superstar new players who are putting in the most on the stats sheets might not be able to be properly compensated for their efforts the way veteran players can already be. 2021 allowed for higher team budgets and increased individual player salaries, so the relationships with salary probably were closer compared to 2020 because teams were allowed to more equitably distribute salaries to those who contributed more.

When looking at what game statistics are better predictors of salary, Points Per Game seems to be the best out of the three that were looked at for both the entire WNBA and veterans only. Points production tends to be associated with the bigger driver in salary amounts—a player may play multiple games and play more minutes, but they need to be able to convert that into points. Of course, this is just one facet of the game and players who do not necessarily score the most points but are defensive specialists or contribute in other ways can also have a salary reflecting that effort. A possible follow up to this project would be to look at more game statistics.
